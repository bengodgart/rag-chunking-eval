Ocean currents are vast rivers of seawater that move heat around the whole planet. Warm surface currents carry tropical heat toward the poles while cold, dense water sinks near the ice and creeps back along the deep sea floor. This slow overturning loop, driven by differences in temperature and salt, is often called the global conveyor belt. By moving heat from the equator toward colder regions, the currents keep coastal climates far milder than they would otherwise be. A shift in these currents can change rainfall and temperature for entire continents within a few decades.
