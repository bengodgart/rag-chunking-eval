Mitochondria are the small compartments inside a cell where most cellular respiration actually takes place. They are often called the powerhouse of the cell because they convert the energy in sugar into adenosine triphosphate, the fuel the rest of the cell runs on. A single busy cell such as a muscle fibre can hold many thousands of mitochondria packed side by side. Strangely, they carry their own small loop of DNA, separate from the DNA in the nucleus, a clue that they were once free living bacteria. That ancient partnership, struck billions of years ago, made complex animal life possible.
